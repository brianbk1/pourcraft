What Is PourCraft?

PourCraft is a free AI-powered cocktail generator built for working bartenders and bar managers, not home hobbyists. List what's behind the bar, and it invents a genuinely original recipe — never a variation on an existing drink — written the way a bar actually specs a menu item: exact amounts, correct terminology, and real technique instructions.

How It Works

  • List your ingredients — up to eight, used as the backbone of the recipe.
  • Set the season — spring, summer, autumn, or winter shapes technique, garnish, and flavor direction.
  • Pick a craft technique (optional) — fat washing, milk washing, oleo saccharum, smoking, rapid infusion, acid adjusting, force carbonation, or saline enhancement.
  • Choose a target color (optional) — the finished drink is built to present that color using real ingredients, never food dye.
  • Set serving size — a single R&D sample, a 12-serving batch for a shift, or a 50-serving event build, with batching math and shelf-life notes included.
  • Add occasion and kitchen context (optional) — tailor the drink to happy hour, a tasting menu, a holiday list, or your current food menu.

Who It's For

Bartenders developing a seasonal menu, bar managers building a batched program for volume service, and consultants pitching a cocktail list to a new venue. Output assumes real bar knowledge — it won't explain what a jigger is, but it will give you a spec you can put straight on a POS.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is PourCraft free?
Yes, completely free, no account required.

Are the recipes actually original?
Each generation invents a new drink rather than pulling from a fixed list — the same ingredients and settings won't produce the same recipe twice.

Can I remix a drink I already generated?
Yes — push any result smokier, sweeter, stronger, lighter, more bitter, or more herbal while keeping its identity intact.

Does it support zero-proof drinks?
Yes, a toggle removes alcohol from every ingredient in the build.

Should I serve a generated recipe without tasting it first?
No — treat every output as a strong starting point that a human still tastes and adjusts before it goes on a menu.
